Executive Summary

Market Position Key Findings Strategic Direction

The online women's fitness coaching market is dominated by brands targeting women over 40 (WarriorBabe) or body-positive/inclusive audiences (Girls Gone Strong). No major brand currently owns the "combat-athlete-turned-coach" positioning for women under 40. Hannah Goldy's UFC credentials, hypothyroid story, and dual-track program (fat loss + muscle building) create a unique positioning that none of the top 5 competitors can replicate. The $297 course price point sits in a strategic gap — premium enough to signal quality, accessible enough to convert social media audiences. With the right design language and social proof strategy, The Gold Standard can carve out a defensible niche as the go-to program for women who want a fighter's physique and mindset without the restrictive, clinical feel of existing programs.

Patterns of the Top 10%

The 5 things all winning sites do that average competitors don't.

Best Practices Competitive Edge Design Patterns
01

Application-Based or Scarcity Funnels

Top brands don't let you "buy now." WarriorBabe requires an application. Girls Gone Strong opens enrollment twice a year. This creates exclusivity, qualifies leads, justifies premium pricing, and makes the prospect feel like they're being selected — not sold to.

02

Massive, Specific Social Proof

WarriorBabe shows 21,800+ transformations and 1,500+ reviews. The bottom competitors have 5-10 generic testimonials. Volume matters, but specificity matters more — "lost 17lbs and 25 inches" beats "great program!" every time. Named clients with real metrics.

03

Proprietary Method Naming

WarriorBabe sells the "WB4 Method." Girls Gone Strong has certification-backed methodology. Top brands don't sell "workouts" — they sell a named system. This prevents direct price comparison and creates perceived uniqueness worth paying for.

04

SaaS-Grade Design Language

Top fitness sites look like tech startups, not fitness blogs. Generous whitespace, professional photography, minimal clutter, clean typography. WarriorBabe and Girls Gone Strong both nail this. Bottom competitors look like Wix templates.

05

Multi-Touchpoint Content Ecosystem

Blog, free courses, certifications, cookbooks, podcasts, YouTube. The top brands create trust-building touchpoints everywhere, converting cold audiences through value before asking for the sale. This is the moat.

Recommended Design Direction

Colors, typography, structure, and animation recommendations backed by competitor data.

Visual Identity UX Strategy Animation

Color Palette

Keep the brand book's Navy (#1C2B3A) + Gold (#E8C96A) + Off-White (#F5F4F0). This already outclasses every competitor's palette. The navy/gold combination signals authority and premium positioning that no competitor currently uses. Add Near Black (#111827) for high-contrast hero sections.

Typography

Anton for headlines creates immediate visual dominance — bolder than any competitor's type choices. Bebas Neue for labels/eyebrows adds tactical, athletic texture. Inter for body keeps everything readable. This three-font system is stronger than WarriorBabe's Oswald/Lato stack.

Page Structure

Single long-form sales page with scroll-triggered sections. Hero with video/3D asset placeholder, credential bar, program overview, curriculum breakdown (tabbed: Fat Loss / Muscle), pricing tiers, FAQ, final CTA. Dark/light section alternation for visual rhythm.

Animation Strategy

GSAP ScrollTrigger for section reveals. Parallax depth on Hannah's photos. Counter animations on stats (12 years, 4 certs). Smooth cinematic feel — Apple/Stripe quality. No competitor uses scroll animation — this is a clear differentiator. prefers-reduced-motion support mandatory.

Social Proof Strategy

Since no testimonials exist yet, build placeholder infrastructure for transformation stories. Include Hannah's own before/after (hypothyroid journey). Use credential badges prominently — UFC, NASM x4, BJJ Black Belt, 12 Years Pro. These ARE the social proof for now.

Conversion Architecture

Built-in checkout (not external redirect) keeps the premium feel. Three tiers visible: Course ($297), Group Coaching ($3,000), 1-on-1 packages ($1,250-$3,500). Primary CTA on every viewport. Sticky nav with CTA button. FAQ section addressing every fake belief from the session notes.

What to Avoid

Based on what the bottom competitors do wrong:

• No emoji in copy or design — Moxie's emoji-heavy approach looks amateur at higher price points.
• No aggressive, clashing colors — Julie Lohre's red/purple/blue creates visual noise.
• No template-looking layouts — The Physical Athlete's basic design kills credibility despite great content.
• No vague testimonials — "Great program!" is worthless. Always use specific metrics when proof becomes available.
• No hidden pricing — At Hannah's price point ($297), showing the price converts better than hiding it behind an application.
